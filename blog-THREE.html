Interviewing Distilled

  • by:medsign
  • date:March 31, 2017
  • 3 Comments

How do you tell a innovator from a pseudo-expert? MIT has been doing research using AI to filter twitter and forums for "catfishers" and pseudo or fake experts. One aspect is that the fakers tend to repeat the real experts. They tend to write in techno-gibberish. It takes a fair bit of thought to be able to explain things to a non-technical audience. The way to filter an expert in an interview is both difficult and easy. First the easy: if you have a genuine expert all they have to do is converse. Someone that actually knows a topic can talk about it. Asking edge questions that you only know if you have done the work is telling. If the interviewee has insight beyond the textbook they obviously know the topic. The issue is that lots of companies are trying to hire experts that they don't have yet. This becomes obvious when the interviewer dives straight into algorithms questions for a math or physics heavy engineering position that might entail a little coding. What does a graph search have do do with a Laplace transform? My first response to that is: do a breadth first or depth first search. A innovator has the same initial resources as everyone else so they will tend to duplicate others solutions but they will also push beyond and come up with new ways to do things. If you always come up with new things you are either a genius or a fool. Most of us think similarly which is why we come up with the same solution. To always come up with a unique solution may be because you either have errors in the solution or it tends toward the brute force slow way to perform the task.
